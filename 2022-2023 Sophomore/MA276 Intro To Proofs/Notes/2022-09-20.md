<u>Proof:</u> Conversely, suppose $\forall A \in \mathcal{F}[\forall B \in \mathcal{K}[[A\cap B \subseteq \bigcup[\mathcal{F}\cap\mathcal{K}]]]$.

Let $x\in[[\bigcup\mathcal{F}]\cap[\bigcup\mathcal{K}]]$. Then $x \in \bigcup\mathcal{F}$ and$x \in \bigcup\mathcal{K}$. This means $\exists A \in \mathcal{F}$ such that $x\in A$ and $\exists B \in \mathcal{K}$ such that $x \in B$. Since $x \in A$ and $x\in B$, $x \in A \cap B$. Since $x \in A \cap B$, by assumption, we know that $A \cap B \subseteq \bigcup[\mathcal{F}\cap\mathcal{K}]$. So $x \in A \cap B$ implies $x \in \bigcup[\mathcal{F}\cap\mathcal{K}]$
